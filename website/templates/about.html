FRC 190 is a robotics team located in Worcester, Massachusetts and funded by Worcester Polytechnic Institute and Jabil Healthcare.

On this page you can learn all about our team’s history, philosophy, quirky traditions and much more.

Team Structure


The team is lead by Colleen Shaver, WPI’s Robotics Resource Center Director. She is supported by the other RRC Staff Amanda Bessette and Francis O’Rourke.

Along with the RRC, group of industry mentors support a variety of team initiatives to ensure success. These include Zach Boyer (project manager, lead designer) and Nick Greeley (project management). Additionally, a rotating group of college mentors from WPI support the team while also learning valuable lessons in engineering project management from our industry leaders.

Our high school students participations primarily hail from the Massachusetts Academy of Math and Science. However, the team welcomes participants from outside the Mass Academy. The goal of this is to help bring robotics to more students in our local community who may not have access. We particularly appreciate freshman/sophomores who want to join the team for multiple years. We are also seeking partnerships with groups like African Community Education (ACE) to support their students.


Team 190 is always looking for new members. If you are a high school student eligible and interested in joining the team email the Robotics Resource Center (first@wpi.edu) and request to be added to the mailing list.

Who is eligible to join as a high school student?

  • Current attendees of Massachusetts Academy of Math and Science at WPI
  • Home-school students (ages 14-18) in the local area
  • Students from a Worcester County high school that does not offer FRC

If you are above high school age and interested in joining as a mentor, email first@wpi.edu for more information.

Our Facilities


Team 190 operates out of a fully equipped shop on the WPI campus. Our shop has a lathe, mill, a horizontal and a vertical band saw, a drill press, CNC router, and powder-coating oven. We also have access to WPI campus resources including a laser cutter, 3D printers, and welding facilities. In addition to shop space, the team has shared access to a large testing space in the school’s Sports and Recreation Center.

This year, the team will be moving its operations to a new building at 15 Sagamore Rd. This space will not only include offices for RRC staff and a dedicated workspace for the team, it will include a larger and more advanced machine shop and a full FIRST field that will be available for community use. We look forward to supporting our fellow FIRST teams by offering them a place to regularly practice and test their robots.

At 15 Sagamore, the team will be joined by other WPI clubs, include Formula SAE, High Powered Rocketry, VEXU, and Combat Robotics. The building also houses advanced faculty labs of WPI researchers.

Our Mascot, Gompei


In 1891, the sophomore class gifted a black goat to WPI. A Japanese student, Gompei Kuwada, was elected to take care of the goat because his initials, G. K., also stood for Goat Keeper. Overtime, the animal became very difficult to maintain, and the goat was eventually beheaded and its head was mounted. The actual Goat’s Head, also known as Gompei’s Head, was eventually replaced with a bronze replica for preservation.

Starting with the Class of 1928, a competition between the freshman and sophomore classes was organized. The classes could earn points through events, such as sports and the tech carnival, and the Goat’s Head was present to the class with the most points. Once a year, the winning class was obliged to show the head publicly to allow the other class to steal it back. Some notable “showings” include hanging the head from a helicopter during a football game and dropping the head from a bridge into a moving convertible. The competition was canceled for some time because of its violent nature; however, it has resumed recently with a new set of rules.

In 1998, FIRST decided that team names and numbers would carry over from year to year. It was at this time Team 190 decided to include “Gompei” in its name.

Team Culture - Crud


Crud is a game that was developed by the American and Canadian Air Forces. It is a competitive team sport played on a billiards table using the cue ball and a striped ball, typically the fifteen. Before the game, two captains are selected, and they pick teams. The primary objective of the game is to keep the fifteen moving by striking it with cue ball. Each player begins with three lives, and they lose a life if they are responsible for letting the ball stop. A player is eliminated when they lose all their lives, and a team is eliminated when all of its players have been eliminated.

Team 190 began playing Crud when Ken Stafford joined the team in 1999. Today, much team spirit comes from Crud. One of new team members’ first experiences is being Crudified, or receiving a Crud name. These names consist of two syllables, are often amusing, and have a connection with the member’s personality, appearance, or behavior. Once or twice a year, team members have the opportunity to order Crud shirts, which have the member’s Crud name and year of Crudification on the back. The team makes a point to play each Saturday during build season at Captain Samuel Eddy’s Crud Emporium, also known as CSECE, or in the WPI Campus Center.
